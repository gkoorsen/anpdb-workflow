# Molecular Dynamics Analysis Methods

Production trajectories were analysed with Python-based workflows using MDTraj, pandas, NumPy, matplotlib, RDKit and OpenMM-derived topology information. Prior to analysis, trajectories were processed with periodic-boundary-aware imaging so that each ligand remained in the same periodic image as its receptor binding site. Protein backbone atoms were used for structural alignment, and trajectory-level quality control was based on backbone RMSD, ligand heavy-atom RMSD, ligand centre-of-mass displacement, ligand-to-pocket distance, residue contact counts and pose-retention descriptors. Per-residue RMSF values were calculated after backbone superposition to identify flexible regions, while ligand pose retention was assessed from the persistence of ligand-protein contacts at 4, 6 and 8 A cutoffs.

Ligand-protein contacts were quantified from heavy-atom distances in each trajectory frame. Contact occupancies were reported as the fraction of analysed frames in which any ligand heavy atom was within the specified cutoff of a residue heavy atom. For publication figures, representative end-point structures were selected from stable production replicates and interaction annotations were derived from the final structures together with the trajectory contact-occupancy tables. Polar contacts were assigned when ligand and protein N/O/S heavy atoms were within 3.5 A; other close contacts were assigned when any ligand-protein heavy-atom pair was within 4.5 A. Two-dimensional ligand interaction schematics were generated with RDKit ligand layouts and annotated with residue identity, final-frame minimum distance and trajectory contact occupancy. Binding-pose schematics were generated by projecting the final ligand and neighbouring binding-site atoms onto their first two principal coordinate axes and overlaying contact annotations.

For SGLT2 systems, direct ligand-protein nonbonded interaction trends were calculated as an additional comparative descriptor. The OpenMM/OpenFF systems were reconstructed from the simulation inputs, and ligand-protein Coulomb and Lennard-Jones interaction energies were evaluated from the NonbondedForce parameters on periodic-boundary-aware trajectories using a 1.0 nm direct-space cutoff. Frames were sampled at a stride of two analysis frames, corresponding to approximately 100 ps for the analysed trajectories. These values were used only as within-target interaction-energy trends and were not interpreted as binding free energies, because they exclude solvent, membrane and entropic contributions and do not include a thermodynamic end-state or alchemical cycle.

The MAO-B simulations used an AMBER-style system to accommodate the FAD-containing enzyme chemistry, whereas SGLT2 and OPRK1 were simulated with the OpenMM/OpenFF workflow. Absolute interaction-energy values were therefore not compared across force-field families. Cross-target interpretation was based on trajectory stability, pose retention and contact persistence rather than absolute force-field-dependent energetic magnitudes. CYP1B1 was excluded from the final comparative MD analysis because it did not pass the target-selection filters. For OPRK1, interpretation considered the crystallographic context of PDB 4DJH, which contains engineered T4 lysozyme fusion segments; equilibrated and production structures were inspected to ensure that the analysed ligand pose remained in the receptor binding region rather than being dominated by the engineered fusion.

All figures and tables were generated from scripted analyses to maintain reproducibility. Per-run trajectory summaries were retained for replicate-level inspection, and publication-level summaries were derived only after checking the individual run tables for unstable trajectories, ligand escape or inconsistent pose-retention behaviour. Interaction schematics and binding-pose figures were exported as PNG for inspection and as SVG/PDF for manuscript preparation.
